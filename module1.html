Module 1: Introduction to Human-Centered Design in Online Learning

  1. EDTECH 201
  2. Content
  3. Module 1
Learning Objectives
  • Understand the meaning and benefits of human-centered design.
  • Understand the current limitations of online learning courses.
  • Identify the difference between traditional online course design and human-centric design.
  • Identify the conditions that lead to students taking online courses and barriers to implementing human-centered design.
Content

Human-centric design aims to “create for human needs and not for unsustainable, manufactured wants” (Small, 2020). But, what does this mean in the context of online education? Designing online courses with humans in mind involves creating courses that prioritize learners' experiences, needs, and empowerment, catering to all, including those underserved by traditional design approaches. Human-centric design aims to encourage engagement, inclusivity, and community building in students. With which “we center the voices of those who are directly impacted by the outcomes of the design process” (Costanza-Chock, 2020). The why becomes clear when we look at the benefits.

Guided by principles outlined in Design Justice, human-centric design enables students to actively participate in their learning, fostering a sense of ownership and agency (Costanza-Chock, 2020). We identify that “the role of the educator is to pose problems, create spaces for the collective development of critical consciousness, [and to] help to develop plans for action to make the world a better place” (Costanza-Chock, 2020). Human-centric design allows for this to take place. We move beyond just presenting information, and instead focus on how students can make an impact, engage with the content, and enjoy it, even when it is presented in a disconnected, online format.

Another benefit of human-centric design is enhanced inclusivity. Acknowledging diverse backgrounds and challenges makes learning accessible and meaningful for all students. Integration of tools which cater to specific needs permits students to connect personally with the material and transform their perspectives.

Digital technology in education often unintentionally reproduces inequality, in large part due to “institutionalized and unconscious bias and social distance between developers and those they seek to serve.”

Sasha Constanza-Chock in Design Justice: Community-Led Practices to Build the Worlds We Need (2020)

Despite the flexibility they offer, online courses often fall short of true accessibility and engagement. Students may find it difficult to engage meaningfully due to life commitments and the misconception that online learning requires less attention or time than traditional courses. On the other hand, the distance between the developers and those they seek to serve creates a gap, making online courses less responsive to student’s needs.

Other limitations include:

  • Struggle to stay engaged, feeling isolated with lack of interaction and community.
  • Challenges with the use of online tools on multiple platforms. Many online courses privilege technologically adept students, marginalizing others without the same access or familiarity (Costanza-Chock, 2020).

Rather than simply valuing the “final” products, regular assessments of student work and of the design process can help improve the overall experience for everyone. Leaving assessments to the end of the process, or just to one or two key moments (such as midterm and the end of the semester), is a mistake.

Sasha Constanza-Chock in Design Justice: Community-Led Practices to Build the Worlds We Need (2020)

Traditional online courses focus primarily on delivering content efficiently and assessing outcomes, usually with a one-size-fits-all approach. Human-centric design, however, centers on holistic student development, fostering a collaborative learning experience that addresses students' diverse needs and perspectives. As such, this course that is designed with humans in mind incorporates engagement with content, gamified elements, and community building. With problem-based learning, students actively engage with tasks, honing critical thinking and collaborative skills.

Problem-based learning: “works best when problems are part of larger, ideally real-world tasks; learners are supported to take ownership of the problem; the task is appropriate to the learner’s level of understanding and ability; the learner must reflect on what is being learned and how they learned it; and the educator encourages the learner to test their ideas in various contexts."

Sasha Constanza-Chock in Design Justice: Community-Led Practices to Build the Worlds We Need (2020)

Students often choose online courses for their perceived flexibility, especially when juggling other commitments. However, institutions frequently overlook the demands on students' time and energy, promoting online education as a “convenient add-on” without accounting for these constraints. Human-centric design considers these factors, and recognizes the need for courses that adapt to the diverse rhythms of students’ lives, providing meaningful engagement without overwhelming them.

We might consider how design decisions that reproduce and/or challenge the matrix of domination are influenced by institutional funding priorities, policies, and practices.

Sasha Constanza-Chock in Design Justice: Community-Led Practices to Build the Worlds We Need (2020)

Developing human-centric online courses requires resources for continuous iteration, feedback, and community engagement—resources that are often limited by institutional funding and priorities. Additionally, there may be a mismatch between perceived flexibility and the real demands of an online course, along with the commitment necessary for success.
